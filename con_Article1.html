A veracity blackout

May 3, 2024 by Jonathan Castillo

On Monday, April 29, 2024, Spain and Portugal were hit by a massive and sudden power outage that affected millions across the Iberian Peninsula. But while technicians worked to restore the power grid, another invisible threat surged through social media and messaging apps: fake news.

Shortly after the blackout began around midday, unverified rumors began circulating online. Some posts falsely claimed that the power failure was the result of a cyberattack from a foreign government, sparking fears of international conflict. Others went further, suggesting that the outage was a prelude to a broader emergency or even a military operation. Despite lacking any official confirmation, these narratives spread rapidly on platforms like X (formerly Twitter), Facebook, and Telegram.

One particularly viral post claimed that NATO forces were being mobilized in response to the outage — a claim quickly denied by both the Spanish and Portuguese defense ministries. Another conspiracy theory alleged that the blackout had been orchestrated by the governments themselves to test population reactions to crisis scenarios, echoing long-standing “deep state” rhetoric seen in global conspiracy circles.

Authorities moved swiftly to counter the misinformation. Spain’s Ministry of the Interior issued a public statement by mid-afternoon, denying any links to foreign aggression or military action. Portugal’s national energy operator, REN, clarified that the outage was likely caused by a technical failure in a high-voltage transmission line connecting both countries — a theory later supported by independent experts.

The incident highlights how quickly disinformation can spread during moments of uncertainty. Experts warn that the combination of fast-moving news cycles, emotional responses, and the algorithm-driven amplification of unverified content makes crises like this especially vulnerable to fake news.

“People crave immediate explanations in times of disruption, and social media often fills that void — not always with the truth,” said Sofia Almeida, a cybersecurity analyst at the University of Lisbon.

While the power was mostly restored within hours, the misinformation left behind may have a longer-lasting impact on public trust. Efforts to improve digital literacy and strengthen rapid-response communication from institutions are once again being called for by media watchdogs and civil society organizations.

The April 29 blackout may soon be remembered not only as an infrastructure failure, but as a warning about the parallel dangers of a misinformed society.
